Drew Seltzer (born June 16, 1981) is an American actor and producer. He is best known for his recurring role as Dale Harmon in the Netflix series Mindhunter, directed by David Fincher, and for his award-winning portrayal of Ray in the independent film Leaving Circadia (2014). Seltzer's career includes roles in major television franchises such as The Walking Dead and FBI, as well as lead performances in independent cinema.

## Early life and education

Seltzer grew up in Media, Pennsylvania, and attended the The Shipley School in Bryn Mawr. He developed an early interest in acting during his teens through his involvement with Hedgerow Theatre in Rose Valley, Pennsylvania, where he appeared in numerous stage productions and later became a resident actor in the summer of 1999. He continued his formal training at Rutgers University, earning a Bachelor of Fine Arts degree. During his senior year, he studied at Shakespeare’s Globe Theatre in London while Sir Mark Rylance was serving as the Globe’s artistic director.

## Career

### Film

Seltzer made his feature film debut in The Men Who Stare at Goats (2009). In 2014, he executive produced and starred in the independent film Leaving Circadia. The film's premiere at the New Jersey Film Festival was profiled by NJ.com, which noted Seltzer's exploration of the "transition from college life" and the "buckling down" of one's thirties. The film received critical attention on the festival circuit, winning Directors Choice at Indie Spirit, Best in Festival at the First Glance Film Festival, and the Verizon People's Choice award at Emerge Film Festival.

His transition to feature films includes playing the role of Rick in the critically acclaimed drama Never Rarely Sometimes Always, directed by Eliza Hittman. The film was reviewed by publications including IndieWire, and was a critical success, winning jury awards at the Sundance Film Festival and the Berlin International Film Festival before being distributed by Focus Features."

In 2026, it was reported by Deadline Hollywood that Seltzer was cast as historical preservationist David McConaughy in the Civil War drama Gettysburg 1863, starring alongside Stephen Lang, Jake Busey, and Bruce Boxleitner. His preparation for the role and his career transition from regional theater to major film productions were the subject of an in-depth profile by The Gettysburg Times.

### Television

In 2019, Seltzer appeared in a recurring role as Dale Harmon in the Netflix series Mindhunter. Working under the direction of David Fincher, Seltzer portrayed the neighbor of protagonist Bill Tench across multiple episodes. Following this, he expanded his television portfolio with roles in major franchises, including the AMC series The Walking Dead: The Ones Who Live and the CBS police procedural FBI. In 2025, Seltzer played a central role in the CBS series Elsbeth as Patrick Palmer. The performance was the subject of a featured interview with Patch Media, which described the episode as one of the season's "most dramatic cases." In the profile, Seltzer discussed filming scenes opposite David Cross, citing Cross's work as a primary inspiration for his own career.

## Theater

Seltzer has performed extensively in Off-Broadway and regional theater. In 2004, he was a member of the original cast of The Franklin Thesis, produced by the Obie Award-winning company Les Freres Corbusier. Directed by Alex Timbers, the production premiered at the New Ohio Theatre and was named "Best of New York 2004" by The Village Voice.

Seltzer's long-standing Off-Broadway credits also include an eight-year tenure in the interactive comedy Tony n' Tina's Wedding, performing at the St. Luke's Theatre on 46th Street in Manhattan's theater district of Times Square.

Regionally, Seltzer has worked with site-specific companies including Theater with a View, where he starred as Howie in a 2017 production of Rabbit Hole. His performance was part of an ensemble described by The Philadelphia Inquirer as "superb" and a "gem of the season." Reviewing the production for DC Theater Arts, critic Neal Newman described Seltzer's performance as "extraordinary."
