Lesław "Les" J. Stawicki (born February 24, 1936) is a Polish-American fencing master and coach. According to USA Fencing, Stawicki is known as the "Father of Parafencing" for his role in establishing the first national wheelchair fencing program in the United States. He served as the Head Coach of the United States Paralympic Fencing Team for 25 years (1991–2016), leading the team through six Paralympic games from Atlanta 1996 to Rio 2016.

## Early life and academic career

Leslaw Stawicki was born in 1936 in Lviv (then part of Poland). He attended the Academy of Physical Education (AWF) in Poznań, where he trained under the Hungarian master János Kevey. Stawicki was a member of the Polish National Sabre Team and eventually earned the title of Zasłużony Trener (Eminent Coach) in Poland. During his European coaching career, he served as a coach for the National Ukrainian Sabre Team (1968–1972) and later the Polish National Sabre Team. Athletes under his direction earned several international honors, including a silver medal at the 1986 World Championships and a bronze at the 1976 World Championships.

## European coaching career (1968–1990)

Prior to emigrating to the United States, Stawicki served as a fencing master in Europe. He was a coach for the Ukrainian National Sabre Team from 1968 to 1972 and later served as a coach for the Polish National Sabre Team from 1973 to 1990. During his tenure in Europe, he coached several high-level international competitors, including Olympic gold medalist Viktor Sidjak and world champion Sergey Prichodko.

## Career in the United States

Following the 1989 World Fencing Championships in Denver, the United States Fencing Association (USFA) recruited several European masters, including Stawicki, to assist in the development of a national coaching education program. Stawicki participated in instructional courses at the U.S. Olympic Training Center in Colorado Springs, which formed the basis of the USFA Coaches College.

Stawicki emigrated to the United States on April 14, 1991, to establish a fencing program in Louisville, Kentucky. In 1992, he founded the Louisville Fencing Center, where he implemented an "integrated" training model that allowed wheelchair fencers and able-bodied fencers to train together. This training model was adopted as the standard for the U.S. national parafencing program in 1995.

In 1995, Stawicki was appointed head coach of the U.S. Paralympic Fencing Team. He led the national program through six consecutive Paralympic cycles: Atlanta (1996), Sydney (2000), Athens (2004), Beijing (2008), London (2012), and Rio (2016). USA Fencing refers to Stawicki as the "Father of Parafencing" in the United States in recognition of his role in establishing the country's first national wheelchair fencing program. Following his retirement from international coaching in 2023, he remained a technical advisor and mentor for the national program.

## Private life

Stawicki resides in Easton, Pennsylvania, with his wife, Lucy. They have three children. His son, Dr. Stanislaw P. Stawicki, is a prominent academic surgeon and researcher who serves as the Chair of the Department of Research & Innovation at St. Luke's University Health Network in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania.

Following his official retirement from full-time coaching at the Louisville Fencing Center in 2023, Stawicki has continued to stay active in the fencing community. He provides remote technical guidance and mentorship to students and coaches, as an advisor via video and digital platforms from his home in Pennsylvania.

## Awards and recognition

* USOPC Recognition: Honored by the United States Olympic & Paralympic Committee for his 25-year tenure and leadership across six Paralympic Games (1996–2016).
* Leszek “Maestro” Stawicki Parafencing Athlete Development Award: In 2022, USA Fencing established this permanent national award in his honor to recognize clubs that demonstrate excellence in the growth and development of wheelchair fencing.
* USA Fencing Hall of Fame (2016): Inducted as a Coach in recognition of his lifetime contributions to the sport and his leadership in the development of wheelchair fencing in the United States.
* Ray Miller Award: In 2011, Stawicki received the Ray Miller Award, an honor bestowed by USA Fencing to recognize individuals who have shown exceptional commitment to the growth of the sport in the United States.
* Competitive Successes: Coached students to over 17 National Championship titles across multiple age categories and weapon disciplines at the Louisville Fencing Center.
