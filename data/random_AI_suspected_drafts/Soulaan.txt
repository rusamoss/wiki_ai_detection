Soulaan is a contemporary ethnonym that emerged in the United States in the early 2020s. The term has appeared in journalism, university-affiliated programming, artistic projects, and student-organizational contexts.

The term has been described as an ethnicity-based identity label rather than a nationality, race, or political designation. Its usage by some emphasizes lineage, historical continuity, and cultural formation rooted in the United States, and it is typically distinguished from broader or more inclusive descriptors such as African-American or Black American.

## Definition

The term Soulaan has been used as an ethnonym for African-American decendents of slavery.

In a 2025 Pitchfork feature discussing rapper Monaleo’s song “Sexy Soulaan,” the term was described as referring to Black Americans whose lineage in the United States predates more recent immigration, distinguishing them from later immigrant-descended populations.

Other media outlets have characterized Soulaan as an ethnonym intended to emphasize historical continuity, ancestry, and cultural formation within the United States. The Metro Record described the term as a name of a people to reclaim a distinct cultural legacy within American history.

#### Soul Autochthonous American

Statements from the creators of Soulaan.com, including Maroc Wallace, describe Soulaan as an acronym for “Soul Autochthonous American.” This incorporates the concept of autochthonism, which is used to express a belief in long-standing cultural and ancestral ties to North America. In this framing, the term is presented as drawing from historical traditions while also encouraging contemporary cultural expression and innovation.

According to them the term emerged as a form of self-identification intended to emphasize cultural and ancestral roots among Black Americans and to distinguish this identity from the label African American. It is intended to reflect a lineage-based understanding of identity rooted in historical continuity within the United States.

In coverage by Billboard, Wallace stated, “We understand that we are from the soil [sic]. We understand that we are people who went through chattel slavery in America, but we also understand that we were here before that as well. We want to acknowledge the sovereignty that stands within the soil.”

## Identity frameworks and continuity

The concept of autogenesis is sometimes used by proponents of Soulaan to describe the cultural continuity that has developed internally within a community, rather than as the result of recent external introduction. In this usage, autogenesis emphasizes the continuity of cultural, social, and ancestral patterns within the Black American experience, including spiritual and community practices.

Anthropological research in population biology has explored how populations may demonstrate long-term continuity within specific geographic regions despite migrations, disruptions, and changing systems of classification. A study indexed in PubMed discussed how ancestral lineages may persist across generations even as cultural and socio-political identities change. Such research is cited in discussions of ancestry and continuity to illustrate that biological lineage does not necessarily align neatly with modern ethno-racial or national categories, and that population continuity can exist independently of contemporary identity labels.

#### Hoodoo as Ethnomedicine

Hoodoo is a body of traditional African-American folk practices associated with the American South. Some commentators have drawn connections between Soulaan-related discourse and cultural practices such as Hoodoo. These associations generally frame them as cultural or symbolic linkages, reflecting shared experiences and continuity in Black American cultural history.

Historically, Hoodoo has encompassed the healing, protection, and guidance techniques that developed within communities shaped by the trans-Atlantic slave trade and the socioeconomic conditions of enslavement and segregation. In biomedical literature, Hoodoo has been described as a ethnomedical system that integrates spirituality, rituals, and culturally specific health practices distinct from formal Western medicine. Within scholarship, these practices are frequently examined in terms of their historical persistence, sociocultural functions, and roles in community-based health strategies, especially among southern Black Americans. Researchers note that Hoodoo combines healing strategies such as herbs, charms, prayer, and rituals. These practices are generally understood as part of the tradition of folk medicine and cultural resilience rather than as formalized religious institutions or healthcare systems.

Hoodoo scholars and practitioners have frequently described the tradition as guarded or selectively transmitted. Academic research has documented longstanding concerns among practitioners regarding secrecy, misuse, and cultural appropriation, noting that Hoodoo knowledge has historically been passed through trusted interpersonal and familial networks rather than open or commercial institutions. Researchers link these practices of discretion to the historical conditions of enslavement, racial surveillance and marginalization, which encouraged controlled transmission of cultural knowledge. Contemporary commentary by Black Americans has similarly emphasized that Hoodoo is a tradition associated with the descendants of enslaved people in the United States. These perspectives often describe the practice as protected or culturally bounded, shaped by communal responsibility rather than universal accessibility. Discussions of Hoodoo as a protected tradition frequently arise in response to concerns about commercialization and misrepresentation, by individuals outside descendant communities.

#### Secret language revival

Tutnese (also known as Tut) is a secret language game tradition associated with African-American communities in the United States. Tutnese developed among enslaved people as a means of private communication and as an aid for spelling and literacy at a time when enslaved literacy was prohibited. The argot is described as a phonetic cipher in which vowels are spoken normally while consonants are replaced by fixed syllables, with pronunciation varying by family and region.

In the early 2020s, Tutnese experienced renewed visibility on social media platforms such as TikTok, where users shared demonstrations of the language and discussed its historical use within Black American communities. Some participants in these online discussions, including individuals who identify with the term Soulaan, have described the practice as part of broader efforts to reconnect with heritage and ancestral traditions. Commentary on these platforms has also included debate over how Tutnese should be shared publicly and who should be considered appropriate custodians of the tradition.

At Tufts University’s Tisch College of Civic Life, the African American Foundations and Roots Organization (AAFRO) has described its mission as celebrating the culture of “Soulaan, or generationally Black American peoples,” in connection with activism, education, and community outreach.

Discussions in which the term Soulaan appears have also occurred within broader debates in higher education concerning race, identity, and institutional governance. During the mid-2020s, university governance bodies at several institutions issued public responses to federal education policies they viewed as affecting academic freedom or institutional autonomy. These debates have been cited as part of the wider context in which lineage-based and cultural identity frameworks are discussed on university campuses, though such policy statements do not reference Soulaan directly as an institutional classification.

## Cultural discourse and symbols

The term Soulaan is frequently discussed in relation to cultural continuity, symbolism, and identity formation among Black Americans. Supporters describe the ethnonym as reflecting cultural indigeneity rooted in long-term historical presence and lineage in the United States. This usage is distinguished from tribal enrollment, territorial claims, or treaty-based Indigenous status, framing Soulaan instead as a conception of identity grounded in ancestry, culture, and historical development.

Soulaan-related discourse has also referenced several flags and symbols associated with Black American identity movements. Some commentators have linked the Black American Heritage Flag, first introduced in 1967, to Soulaan due to its early use as a symbol of Black American lineage and continuity. The Black American Heritage Flag predates the emergence of the Soulaan ethnonym and is described as a general cultural symbol rather than as an official emblem of any specific identity framework.

In later years, individuals associated with Soulaan.com introduced a distinct flag intended to represent the ethnonym and its associated cultural framework. According to statements by its creators, the design draws inspiration from earlier Black American symbolism while incorporating new elements intended to reflect cultural continuity, lineage awareness, and creative innovation. The flag is used in artistic, educational, and community settings and is not intended as a national, territorial, or treaty-based symbol.

Soulaan-related discussions have also referenced the Black American Flag, introduced in 2003, which features a black field and a single white star commonly interpreted as symbolizing the North Star. In broader cultural discourse, this flag is associated with themes of self-determination, migration history, and freedom. References to the Black American Flag within Soulaan-associated spaces are generally framed as part of a wider constellation of Black American cultural symbols rather than as an exclusive or official emblem of the Soulaan ethnonym.

Beyond identity discourse, Soulaan has appeared as a cultural and artistic motif. In 2025, The Oxford American published “Soulaan Femme,” a creative work that used the term as a thematic title, illustrating its use within contemporary Black American arts and literary expression.

The term has also appeared in visual and creative arts. In 2025, an undergraduate student at the Penny W. Stamps School of Art & Design at the University of Michigan produced an animation titled “Soulaan,” which the artist described as engaging with themes of cultural memory, displacement, and historical continuity within Southern Black communities.

Soulaan has additionally been referenced in music journalism and popular culture reporting, particularly in coverage of hip-hop artists and fashion creators who incorporate the term into their artistic narratives. The term has also appeared within creative works themselves; rapper Monaleo uses the word in her 2025 studio album Who Did the Body.

The term has also appeared in student-led innovation and entrepreneurship contexts. In 2025, during the Gaels Ignite startup pitch competition hosted by Saint Mary’s College of California, a student project titled “Soulaan,” described as a Black art anthology and digital portal, was among the ventures recognized by the event’s judges.

## Reception and criticism

Several online media outlets have highlighted the emergence of Soulaan in public discourse. In 2025, Yahoo Finance described Soulaan as a “breakthrough ethnonym” that had gained visibility on social media platforms, noting its circulation beyond niche discussions into broader cultural conversations.

Commentary on the term has included differing perspectives regarding its status and significance. Some commentators have expressed skepticism about classifying Soulaan as a distinct ethnic group, noting that it has not been formally recognized by governmental or statistical institutions. Others have characterized the term as an example of contemporary identity formation shaped by cultural, historical, and political discourse, rather than as an established demographic classification.

Additional discussion has focused on how lineage-based ethnonyms such as Soulaan relate to existing racial and ethnic categories, including African-American. Some writers have suggested that such terms may overlap with or complicate existing classifications, while others interpret their emergence as part of ongoing debates in the United States concerning self-identification, naming practices, and historical memory.
