Masho Gagar (also spelled Masho Gaggar) is a historic village in the southern outskirts of Peshawar city 13km away from the provincial capital, in [[Peshawar District], Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan.The Spoken Language in the village is Pashto and its people belong from the Pashtun tribe Mohmand

## History

Masho Gagar is an ancient settlement that gained prominence during the 16th-century migrations of the Ghoryakhel Pashtuns. According to the Gazetteer of the Peshawar District, the village became a primary seat for the Kudrizi sub-clan of the Kuz (Lower) Mohmand tribe.

## History

### Origins and Early Settlement

Masho Gagar is an ancient settlement whose name reflects the typical Pashtun clan-based village nomenclature of the Peshawar Valley, where communities formed around extended patrilineal family groups. The inhabitants belong to the Kudrizi sub-clan of the Kuz (Lower) Mohmand, a branch of the Ghoryakhel Pashtuns who migrated into the Peshawar plain during the 16th century, displacing the earlier Dilazak Pashtun inhabitants of the valley.

By the 17th century, Masho Gagar had emerged as a notable settlement within the Kuz Mohmand territory, serving as a seat of the Kudrizi sub-clan. The village also became associated with Sufi religious life during this period, a tradition that would shape its cultural character for centuries.

The earliest firm documentary evidence of the village's prominence comes from the 18th century, through its association with the classical Pashto Sufi poet Abdul Hamid Baba (d. c. 1732), whose birthplace the village is widely identified as in both colonial and modern scholarship.

## Geography and Land

The village is situated in a fertile alluvial plain irrigated by the Bara River. Historically, control over the irrigation channels of the Bara River made Masho Gagar a strategically and economically important site. The area is highly fertile and is known for floriculture, particularly the production of roses for the Peshawar market, as well as wheat and vegetables. The village is bordered by several prominent localities, including Masho Khel, Badaber, Suleman Khel, and Mattani.

### Colonial Period

Under British colonial administration, following the annexation of the Peshawar Valley in 1849 after the Second Anglo-Sikh War, Masho Gagar was incorporated into the settled districts of the North-West Frontier as part of systematic land revenue settlement operations. British settlement officers conducted detailed surveys of the area, documenting tribal landholding patterns, irrigation rights over the Bara River channels, and the agricultural character of the village.

The 1897–98 Gazetteer of the Peshawar District records the southern Peshawar plain, including the area of Masho Gagar, as highly fertile agricultural land irrigated by the Bara River, known for its wheat cultivation and floriculture. British records also noted the strategic importance of controlling the Bara River irrigation channels, which governed the productivity of villages throughout this part of the district.

A notable event in the village's colonial history occurred in 1847, when the people of Masho Gagar refused to pay revenue to Major George Lawrence of the British Raj, marking one of the early instances of local resistance to colonial revenue demands in the Peshawar Valley.

### Post-Independence

Following Pakistan's independence in 1947, Masho Gagar became part of the North-West Frontier Province, later renamed Khyber Pakhtunkhwa in 2010 under the 18th Amendment to the Constitution of Pakistan. The village has remained primarily agricultural, with its economy continuing to centre on floriculture and wheat farming as in the colonial period.

In 2019, the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa government established a Kisan market (farmers' market) in Masho Gagar to allow local growers to sell produce directly to consumers, bypassing traditional middlemen.

## Demographics

### Population and Ethnic Composition

The inhabitants of Masho Gagar are overwhelmingly Pashtun, belonging to the Mohmand tribe, consistent with the ethnic composition of the broader southern Peshawar plain. Pashto is the primary spoken language, reflecting the cultural and linguistic homogeneity typical of Mohmand settlements in Peshawar District. The Mohmand of this area belong specifically to the Kuz (Lower or Plain) Mohmand division, who settled the flat agricultural lands of the southern Peshawar Valley in contrast to the Bar (Upper or Hill) Mohmand who inhabit the mountainous areas further north and west.

No significant presence of other ethnic communities is documented in the village, though the proximity to urban Peshawar means some interaction with the city's more diverse population occurs through market and employment activity.

### Social Structure

Social organisation in Masho Gagar follows the patrilineal clan structure characteristic of Pashtun tribal society, centred on the khel system of lineage groups. The core tribal affiliation is to the Kudrizi sub-clan of the Kuz Mohmand, a lineage documented in 19th-century British scholarship on the Mohmand tribe. Community life is governed by Pashtunwali, the unwritten ethical code emphasising honour (nang), hospitality (melmastia), asylum (nanawatai), and justice (badal).

Dispute resolution is conducted through the jirga, an assembly of male elders which adjudicates matters using customary precedent rather than formal state law. The hujra, a communal guesthouse, serves as a centre of male social life, hosting guests, poetry recitation, and community discussions. A notable feature of Masho Gagar's social history is the presence of a Sahibzada religious family — descendants of the Sufi scholar Akhund Momin Muhammad — who have historically occupied a position of religious prestige within the local Mohmand community.

## Economy

### Agriculture and Floriculture

Agriculture is the backbone of Masho Gagar's economy. The village is situated on the fertile alluvial plain of the Bara River, which supports intensive cultivation through an ancient system of irrigation channels. The area is particularly noted for floriculture — the commercial cultivation of flowers — which has historically provided a significant source of income for local farming households.

Major crops include roses, which are harvested for the Peshawar flower market, alongside wheat and vegetables. In 2019, the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa government's establishment of a Kisan market in Masho Gagar was intended to improve returns for local farmers by enabling direct sales to consumers, reducing dependence on intermediary traders who had historically captured much of the profit margin from the flower and vegetable trade.

### Other Economic Activities

Beyond farming, residents engage in labour, trade, and public sector employment, facilitated by the village's proximity — approximately 13 kilometres — to Peshawar city. The village's location along the southern Peshawar corridor has brought it into the orbit of infrastructure development projects, including the National Highway Authority's Southern Link Road, which passes through the area and has brought both economic implications and community concerns about agricultural land acquisition.

## Infrastructure

### Education

The village hosts public educational facilities serving local children, including GPS Awal Khan Korona, a government primary school documented in official Khyber Pakhtunkhwa education records. As with many villages in the southern Peshawar periphery, educational access has been affected by militant violence in previous years, with schools in the broader Badaber–Masho Gagar area targeted during the height of insurgent activity in the region.

### Healthcare

Basic healthcare is provided through local health facilities serving the Masho Gagar area, consistent with the district-level primary healthcare infrastructure of rural Peshawar. Residents requiring specialist treatment travel to major hospitals in Peshawar city, including Hayatabad Medical Complex and Khyber Teaching Hospital, approximately 10–13 kilometres away.

### Transportation and Utilities

Masho Gagar is connected to Peshawar city and surrounding villages by rural roads forming part of the southern Peshawar road network. The National Highway Authority's Southern Link Road project passes through the village area, forming part of a broader connectivity initiative in the Peshawar southern corridor. Electricity is supplied by the Peshawar Electric Supply Company (PESCO). Water supply is provided through community schemes, and the village's agricultural land is irrigated primarily through channels drawing from the Bara River.

## Culture and Heritage

### Sufi Tradition

Masho Gagar occupies a notable place in the Sufi and literary heritage of the Peshawar Valley. The village contains the shrine of Akhund Momin Muhammad, a Chishti Nizami Sufi figure described on his gravestone as Khwaja-ul-Khwajagan (Master of Masters), whose descendants — the Sahibzada family — have historically held religious prestige within the local Mohmand community.

The village is most widely known in Pashto literary history as the birthplace of Abdul Hamid Baba (d. c. 1732), one of the classical poets of the Pashto language. The 19th-century British officer and Pashto scholar H.G. Raverty identified him as having been born at Masho Gagar, belonging to the Kudrizi clan of the Mohmand tribe, and described him as the Sheikh Saadi of Pashto literature. His grave lies in an ancient graveyard adjacent to the village.

### Abdul Hamid Baba

Abdul Hamid Baba (عبدالحميد ماشو ګګر; d. c. 1732), also known as Hamid of Mashogagar, was a Pashtun Sufi poet and philosopher born in Masho Gagar in the second half of the 17th century. He received his education in Peshawar and became a figure of considerable intellectual stature, with students travelling from surrounding districts to study under him. His poetry, written in Pashto, is characterised by Sufi mysticism, philosophical inquiry, and moral reflection. He is considered a significant figure in classical Pashto literature, comparable in standing to contemporaries such as Rahman Baba.

Despite his historical importance, Hamid Baba received comparatively little institutional recognition in Pakistan. His grave in Masho Gagar fell into serious disrepair and was described as lying in ruins in 2012, prompting calls from local literary organisations for reconstruction and official recognition. A 40-minute documentary on his life and works, produced by journalist Arif Shafi, was completed shortly before Shafi's death in a suicide attack in Peshawar in 2013.

## Notable People

* Abdul Hamid Baba (d. c. 1732) — classical Pashto Sufi poet and moralist, born in Masho Gagar.
* Akhund Momin Muhammad — 17th-century Chishti Nizami Sufi scholar, buried in Masho Gagar; progenitor of the local Sahibzada family.
* Gulzar Khan (1938–2017) — former Member of the National Assembly, buried in his ancestral graveyard in Masho Gagar.
* Shandana Gulzar Khan — politician and Member of the National Assembly.
