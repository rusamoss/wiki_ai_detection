The Pitt Review, formally titled Learning Lessons from the 2007 Floods, was an independent review commissioned by the Government of the United Kingdom following severe flooding in England and Wales in 2007. The review was chaired by Sir Michael Pitt and published in June 2008. It examined the causes of the flooding, the effectiveness of preparation and response arrangements, and options for improving flood risk management in the United Kingdom.

## Background

In the summer of 2007, England and Wales experienced prolonged periods of heavy rainfall, resulting in widespread flooding across multiple regions. The flooding affected tens of thousands of homes and businesses, disrupted transport networks, and caused failures to essential services, including electricity and water supplies. The events were among the most extensive flood incidents in the United Kingdom in recent decades. In response, the government commissioned an independent review to examine lessons arising from the floods and to make recommendations for future policy, planning, and emergency management.

## Establishment and remit

Sir Michael Pitt, then Chair of the South West Strategic Health Authority, was appointed to lead the review. The remit of the review included:

* assessing the scale and causes of the 2007 flooding;
* reviewing the effectiveness of flood risk management, emergency response, and recovery arrangements;
* identifying responsibilities across national and local organisations; and
* making recommendations to improve resilience to future flooding.
* The review gathered evidence from government departments, local authorities, emergency services, infrastructure operators, professional bodies, and members of the public.

## Findings

The Pitt Review identified a range of issues related to flood preparedness and response. These included limitations in forecasting and warning arrangements, uncertainty over organisational responsibilities, and challenges in coordinating emergency responses during complex flood events. The review also highlighted the role of surface water flooding and drainage system capacity in contributing to the impacts of the 2007 floods. It noted that such risks had received less attention than river and coastal flooding in previous policy and planning frameworks.

## Recommendations

The review made 92 recommendations aimed at improving flood risk management and resilience. Key areas addressed included:

* clearer allocation of responsibilities between national and local bodies;
* improvements to flood forecasting, warning, and public communication;
* strengthened emergency planning and response arrangements;
* greater consideration of surface water flooding; and
* integration of flood risk considerations into spatial planning and development control.

The recommendations were intended to inform future legislation, policy development, and operational practice.

## Government response

The UK Government published a formal response to the Pitt Review later in 2008, accepting the majority of its recommendations either in full or in principle. The response set out a programme of actions and reforms to be taken forward across government and public bodies.

Subsequent policy and legislative measures were influenced by the review, including changes to institutional responsibilities for flood risk management and emergency planning.

## Relationship to later legislation

Several recommendations from the Pitt Review informed the development of the Flood and Water Management Act 2010. The Act introduced Lead Local Flood Authorities in England with responsibilities for managing local flood risks, including surface water and groundwater flooding, and sought to improve coordination between risk management authorities. Not all measures discussed in the review were implemented immediately, and some aspects were introduced through policy rather than statute.

## Legacy

The Pitt Review is frequently referenced in discussions of flood risk management reform in the United Kingdom. It contributed to a broader recognition of surface water flooding as a significant risk and to changes in governance and planning frameworks following the 2007 floods. The review continues to be cited in policy documents, professional guidance, and academic literature relating to flood risk, emergency management, and climate adaptation.
