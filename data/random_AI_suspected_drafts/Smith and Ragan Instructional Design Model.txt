### Smith and Ragan Instructional Design Model

Overview

The Smith and Ragan Instructional Design Model is a systematic approach to designing instruction based on analysis, strategy and evaluation. Created by Patricia L. Smith and Tillman J. Ragan, this model provides a practical framework for planning, developing and accessing instruction in education and training settings (Smith & Ragan, 2005). The model emphasizes three main phases: analysis, strategy development and evaluation, each serving an essential role in shaping learning experiences (Seels & Glasgow, 1998). The model’s goal is to ensure instructional designers are guided by an understanding of learners, learning goals and learning context. It links theoretical foundations of learning to design processes used by instructional designers in schools, universities and workplace learning environments (Smith & Ragan, 1999). This model has been classified as being systems oriented (Gustafson & Branch, 2002). This classification indicates that the model is most effective for developing large amounts of instruction such as an entire course or curriculum (Raible, 2020). In their survey of Instructional Development Models (2002), (Gustafson & Branch, 2002), suggest this model may be useful for those interested in the psychology of instructional design.

History and Development

Smith and Ragan’s instructional design model draws from the work of several major theorist in learning and instructions. Robert M. Gagne developed the Conditions of Learning which described how instructional events influence cognitive processes and learning outcomes (Gagne, 1985).. David Merrill introduced the Component Display Theory, a framework that organizes instructional; content and performance to enhance learner comprehension (Merrill, 1983). Charles M. Reigeluth contributed the Elaboration Theory, which emphasizes sequencing instructions from simple to complex to promote learner-centered understanding (Reigeluth, 1999). They credited Gange, Merrill, and Reigeluth for foundational concepts linking instructional events, learning processes, and measurable outcomes (Smith & Ragan, 2005)

The first edition of Instructional Design by Smith and Ragan, published in 1993, outlined a structured design process that linked instructional theory with real-world application. The second edition which was published in 1999 expanded on this foundation by refining the analysis phase and providing detailed guidance for designing instructional strategies. The third edition published in 2005 reflected advances in cognitive science, multimedia learning, and online education. The model emerged during a period of increasing demand for flexible instructional approaches in distance and technology-enhanced learning and reflects their philosophic belief that applying systematic, problem-solving process can result in effective learner-centered instruction (Gustafson & Branch, 2002).

Developers

Patricia L. Smith is a professor in the Instructional Psychology and technology programme at the University of Oklahoma. She received her Ph.D. in Instructional Systems from Florida State university in 1982. Dr. Smith is the author of two books, and numerous journal articles, technical reports and book chapters on computer-based instruction and instructional design. To date she has thirteen publications.

“She served as the president of the Research and Theory Division and a member of the board of directors of that division as well as the Division of Instructional Development. She has also served as co-chair of the Professors of Instructional Design and Technology conference and as president of the Instructional Technology Special Interest Group of the American Education Research Association” (Smith & Ragan, 2005).

Tillman J. Ragan is a professor Emeritus in the Instructional Psychology and Technology program at the University of Oklahoma. He received his PH.D. in Instructional Technology from Syracuse University in 1970. Dr. Ragan is the author of five books and numerous articles, technical reports, and chapters on instructional technology, and he has been a columnist for Educational Technology magazine.

“He has served on committees and has been president of the Research and Theory Division and of the Division of Instructional Development of the Association for Education Communications and Technology, vice-president of the International Visual Literacy Association, and co-chair of the Professors of Instructional Design Technology conference” (Smith & Ragan, 2005).

Model Description

The Smith and Ragan Instructional Design Model includes three main phases: Analysis, Strategy and Evaluation. These three phases provide the conceptual framework for the eight steps that comprise the Instructional Design Process (Raible, 2020). The model follows an eight-step process that includes analyzing the learning context, analyzing the learners, analyzing the learning task, assessing learner performance, developing instructional strategies, producing instructions, conducting evaluation and revising instructions. Each step builds on the previous one to ensure that learning outcomes align with learner needs and the instructional goal. Smith and Ragan discussed in their text that there is a natural tendency by designers to view the instructional design process as linear. However, they explained that the activities associated with the development of instructional design as being concurrent. Thee steps within each phase are often interwoven in such a way that changes in one phase cause the instructional designer to make changes in other phases.

The first phase is the analysis and assessment phase and focuses on understanding the learning context and needs. It considers the learner’s needs, the learning context and the learning goals, serving as the foundation for the instructional design process (Institute, 2024). The first step in the contextual analysis is a needs analysis where instructional designers use a needs assessment to determine if the development of instructions and subsequent learning will result in the desired outcome.

During this phase instructional designers identify learner characteristics and needs, environmental factors and the instructional goals. It involves who the learners are by considering their age, background, prior knowledge, learning styles and abilities to ensure that instruction is relevant and effective. It also includes determining what the learners are expected to achieve by the end of the instructional experience through clear learning goals. Additionally, the analysis phase assesses the learning context, whether classroom, workshop or online to determine the best strategies, materials and assessments. The analysis phase ensures that the instruction is learner-centered.

Smith and Ragan (2005) outlined three types of needs assessment models to be utilized in the first phase. The Problem Model addresses situation where a problem requires correction. The Innovation which is responsible for introducing new skills. The Discrepancy Model which is responsible for evaluating a course or programme to see if it meets the required standards. The output of a needs assessment should be a list of learner goals highlighting what the learners would be able to do after instruction.

The second phase is the strategy development phase and focuses on creating instructional strategies and producing instructions effectively. It considers the organizations delivery management, aligning learning outcomes with teaching methods and media selection. During this phase the instructional designer uses findings from the analysis phase to plan instructional strategies that support the learning goals and meet learner needs. This phase focuses on choosing the most effective instructional strategies, materials and media to achieve the intended learning outcomes (Raible, 2020).

In this phase, the instructional designer selects suitable teaching methods that align with the learning goals and learner needs. The instructional designer then proceeds to develop the content, ensuring they meet the objectives and engage learners. The teaching method and content work together to create organized and effective learning experiences.

The third phase is the evaluation phase and focuses on measuring the effectiveness of the instruction (Raible, 2020). Evaluation in the form of formative and summative assessments, takes place during and after instruction to ensure quality and improvement. Formative assessments examine the effectiveness of the instructional design during development, while summative assessments measures learning outcomes after implementation.

“Although the model is presented as linear, its authors point out that the phases often happen concurrently, and considerations in one phase may (and often do) overlap with those in another phase” (Hilgart, Ritterband, Thorndike, & Kinzie, 2012). Smith and Ragan explain that in practice, several steps of the model often occur at the same time and require simultaneous attention (Smith & Ragan, 2005). # Applications

The Smith and Ragan model is gaining wider use in instructional technology, particularly among those interested in the cognitive psychology base of Instructional design (Gustafson & Branch, 2002). This instructional design model can be applied in education, corporate training and healthcare. The model can also be adapted for web-based scaffolding to support learner independence and self-regulation. Instructional designers can use structured online tools and prompts to guide learners through each of the three main phases: analysis, strategy development and evaluation, (Dabbagh, 2003).

# Research and Reception

Research literature describes the Smith and Ragan model as a comprehensive, practical framework for analyzing context, specifying outcomes, selecting strategies and conducting assessments (Gustafson & Branch, 2002). The Smith and Ragan model differs from other instructional design models by placing assessment development the first phase (Baturay, 2008). According to Smith and Ragan, it is important that designers think about assessing learners’ performance before developing instructional strategy. It also differs from simple linear process descriptors by laying out explicit steps that link goals, strategies, delivery, and assessment in an iterative process (Smith & Ragan, 2005).

Conclusion

The Smith and Ragan Instructional Design Model offers a clear, structured process for creating effective learning experiences. It links theory with practice in three key phases: analysis, strategy development, and evaluation. Each phase build on the other to align learner needs and instructional outcomes. The model’s emphasis on learner analysis, context and assessments, promote purposeful and measurable learning outcomes.

Its iterative structure build into the three phases supports continuous improvement through evaluation and feedback. This approach makes the model suitable for designing instructions across education, corporate training, and online learning environments. By following the phases of this instructional design model instructional designers can produce systematic, evidence-based learning that can respond to both cognitive and contextual needs.
