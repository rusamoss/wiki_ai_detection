The Washington Academy of Sciences (WAS) is a nonprofit scientific organization in Washington, D.C., United States. The academy was established in 1898 to serve as a coordinating body that brought together scientific and engineering societies in the Washington metropolitan area.

Starting in 1899, the Washington Academy of Sciences began publishing the Proceedings of the Washington Academy of Sciences (1899–1911). In 1911, the publication was renamed to Journal of the Washington Academy of Sciences.

## History

The Washington Academy of Sciences was founded by the Joint Commission of Scientific Societies of Washington. The commission fulfilled the goal of establishing a single organization to coordinate scientific meetings in the Washington D.C. area. The academy was formally incorporated in the District of Columbia on February 18, 1898. The founders included Alexander Graham Bell and Samuel Langley, Secretary of the Smithsonian Institution. The purpose of the new academy was to encourage the advancement of science and “to conduct, endow, or assist investigation in any department of science.” The background, origin, and early years of the WAS are documented in the Smithsonian Institution Archives. The first fifty years of the WAS are described in Science (1948).

The founding affiliated societies were the Anthropological Society of Washington; Biological Society of Washington; Chemical Society of Washington; Entomological Society of Washington; National Geographic Society; Geological Society of Washington; Medical Society of the District of Columbia; and Philosophical Society of Washington.

## Organization and structure

The Washington Academy of Sciences functions as a federation of scientific and engineering societies in the Washington, D.C. region. Its governance is managed by a Board of Managers composed of delegates from affiliated societies alongside elected officers and at-large members. There are multiple committees that oversee the junior academy, the publication of the journal (including editing and formatting), membership, fundraising, education/outreach and finance. The WAS is an affiliated academy of the National Association of Academies of Science.

### Affiliated societies

At its founding in 1898 the academy incorporated eight original affiliated societies: the Anthropological Society of Washington; Biological Society of Washington; Chemical Society of Washington; Entomological Society of Washington; National Geographic Society; Geological Society of Washington; Medical Society of the District of Columbia; and Philosophical Society of Washington. Over time the number of affiliated societies has expanded; the academy's website states the federation comprises “over sixty scientific societies.” A roster of current affiliates is published in the Journal of the Washington Academy of Sciences and on the academy's website.

## Activities and programs

The Washington Academy of Sciences hosts regular lectures, meetings and symposia for scientists and engineers in the Washington, D.C. region. Each year the academy organizes an annual awards banquet, typically held in May, where new fellows are awarded and awards, such as Distinguished Career in Science, Excellence in Research, and Teaching, are distributed. William Daniel Phillips (Nobel Prize, Physics, 1997) credits an early WAS award as being "especially important to me as a young scientist."

### Junior Academy of Sciences

The Junior Academy of Sciences is the educational-outreach arm of the academy, active since the 1940s, serving secondary-school students in the District of Columbia, Maryland and Virginia. The program supports local STEM fairs by providing professional scientists to judge student research projects and awarding certificates of achievement. For example, since 2017 the Junior Academy has participated in the annual STEM fair at Blair High School in Montgomery County, Maryland. The Junior Academy also engages with robotics teams and maker-space initiatives; the academy's website reports partnerships with FIRST® Robotics and maker-spaces in the region.

## Publications

### Proceedings of the Washington Academy of Sciences (1899–1911)

The academy's first serial publication, the Proceedings of the Washington Academy of Sciences, began in 1899 and continued through 1911.

### Journal of the Washington Academy of Sciences (1911–present)

In 1911 the academy launched the current journal, Journal of the Washington Academy of Sciences, replacing the Proceedings. The journal is peer-reviewed and is currently published quarterly. The previous issues of the journal are available through JSTOR.

### Additional Materials

The archive of the academy's records includes correspondence about special publications, such as a monograph entitled *The Parasitic Cuckoos of Africa* and a symposium volume titled *Microsomal Particles and Protein Synthesis*. The academy has published numerous YouTube videos

## Notable members and fellows

Membership in the Washington Academy of Sciences has included scientists, engineers and educators active in the Washington, D.C., region.

Some of the academy's members and Fellows include:

* Charles Doolittle Walcott – Paleontologist, Director of the Smithsonian Institution, and president of the Washington Academy of Sciences.
* Cyrus Adler – Scholar and librarian, served as president of the academy.
* Otto L. Meinzer – Ground-water hydrologist.
* William Healey Dall – Malacologist and.
* Austin Hobart Clark – Zoologist associated with the Smithsonian Institution and the academy.
* Harry Diamond (engineer) – Engineer and inventor; recipient of the academy's Engineering Award in 1941.
* William Daniel Phillips – Physicist and Nobel Prize laureate in Physics (1997) for development of laser cooling and trapping of atoms; recognized by the academy.
* John C. Mather – Astrophysicist and Nobel Prize laureate in Physics (2006); the academy's awards list recognizes his contribution in the Physical Sciences category in 2006.
* Vint Cerf – Internet pioneer.
* Laurie E. Locascio – Former Director of the National Institute of Standards & Technology (NIST).

## Headquarters

The Washington Academy of Sciences offices are located in the American Associate for the Advancement of Sciences building in Washington, D.C.
