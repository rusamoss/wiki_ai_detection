The Famous 4th Street Delicatessen (also known as Famous 4th Street Deli or simply Famous) is a kosher-style Jewish delicatessen in the Queen Village neighborhood of Philadelphia, Pennsylvania. Established in 1923 by Hungarian Jewish immigrant Sam Auspitz and his brothers, it is one of the oldest continuously operating delis in the city and is renowned for its traditional Jewish fare. Known for oversized sandwiches, house-cured pastrami and corned beef, matzah ball soup, and fresh baked goods, the deli serves traditional Jewish fare but is not certified kosher. The deli has remained a cultural landmark, political gathering spot, and community hub, particularly on Election Days, attracting politicians, locals, and tourists alike. It celebrated its centennial in 2023 and has undergone two ownership changes in the 21st century while preserving its original character.

## History

Born in 1906 in Hungary, Sam Auspitz immigrated at age 13 with his mother and six siblings. He worked in a deli as a child for $1, a corned beef sandwich, and soda. He opened the first Famous Deli at 16 in 1923 with $300. With three brothers, they operated five Famous Delis. The brothers divided the partnership, and Sam took the Bainbridge store. The Famous 4th Street Delicatessen originated as a corner grocery and takeout counter, amid Philadelphia's garment district populated by Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe. It was founded as "Auspitz Brothers Delicatessen, famous for fine foods," reflecting the era's bustling pushcart markets and textile trade. The establishment initially catered to the local Jewish community with kosher staples like corned beef, pastrami, and blintzes, embodying the immigrant hustle that built many of the city's early businesses.

In the 1950s, under Sam Auspitz's management, the deli expanded to include table service, transforming it into a full-service restaurant with its signature black-and-white tiled floors, tin ceilings, and enclosed porch overlooking the neighborhood. This period marked its rise as a national name, drawing a diverse clientele from longshoremen to high-profile figures like Mayor Frank Rizzo and mobster Angelo Bruno. By the 1970s, it had become a fixture for political gatherings, particularly on Election Days, a tradition that began around 1975 with Democratic Party leaders like Pete Camiel and David Glancey.

The deli adapted to modern challenges, introducing online ordering in 2019, which accounted for 65% of sales during the COVID-19 pandemic. It remains one of the few surviving authentic Jewish delis in Philadelphia, amid a broader decline from dozens in the mid-20th century to just a handful by the 2020s, as Jewish populations shifted to suburbs.

## Ownership

### Auspitz Family and Origins

The deli was founded by the Auspitz brothers, Jewish immigrants who established multiple delis in Philadelphia's Jewish neighborhoods. The brothers, including figures like Uncle Arthur, Aunt Celie, and Uncle Joe, opened their first location for $300 at 31st and York streets, expanding to five sites by the early 20th century. The 4th Street branch, the most successful, was part of this network and served the garment district's immigrant workforce. The Auspitz family embodied the entrepreneurial spirit of Eastern European Jews fleeing turmoil, building businesses through hard work and community ties.

#### Family Genealogy

The Auspitz family originated from Hungary in Eastern Europe. Sam Auspitz, a key figure in the deli's history, was born around 1906 in Hungary and immigrated to the United States at age 13, around 1919, following his father who had arrived earlier to prepare for the family. Sam's early life in Hungary involved living in a sod hut and riding oxcarts. Upon arrival, he worked in a delicatessen before opening his first deli at age 19 with $300 at 31st and York streets. He later partnered with his three brothers to operate five Famous delis across the city.

Sam's brothers included Arthur (married to Aunt Celie, operating a deli on Ogontz Avenue) and Joe (operating on Haverford Avenue), along with at least one other unnamed brother. The family divided the partnership in the early 1950s, with Sam taking the Bainbridge Street (4th Street) location. Sam and his family supported Israel's independence, with his brothers sending ambulances overseas and Sam hiding ammunition and rifles under the deli's floorboards.

Sam married and had two sons: David and Jack Auspitz. The family lived in a large stone house at 66th and 12th streets in East Oak Lane, Philadelphia, without central air conditioning, where the brothers shared childhood memories like sleeping near their parents' window unit on hot nights. Sam's older son, Jack, was sent to college through his father's generosity. Sam died in 1989 at age 83.

David Auspitz, Sam's son, married Janie Auspitz, with whom he co-founded the Famous 4th Street Cookie Company. David has at least one son, whose wedding was noted in 2007. David's older brother, Jack, attended Central High School in Philadelphia and was involved in civil rights activities, inviting David to the March on Washington.

Sam Auspitz, one of the key family members, took over the 4th Street location in the mid-20th century. His career focused on expanding the deli's offerings, adding table service in the 1950s, and fostering an inclusive atmosphere where everyone—from governors to garbage collectors—received equal treatment. Sam was also involved in supporting Israel's independence, hiding ammunition under the deli's floorboards. He managed the business until his illness in the 1970s and death in 1989.

David Auspitz, Sam's son, assumed control in 1970 after leaving an investment banking career in Washington, D.C., to care for his father. He operated the deli for 35 years, working 80 hours a week, seven days a year, including holidays. Under David, the deli solidified its reputation, and he introduced the Famous 4th Street Cookie Company around 1978, which became a separate venture at Reading Terminal Market. David sold the deli in 2005 to Russ Cowan but retained rights to host Election Day events.

### Subsequent Owners

#### The Cowan Era (2005–2024)

In 2005, Russ Cowan, acquired the business. He refurbished the space, refined the menu for consistency, and enhanced food quality while preserving traditions. Cowan's tenure included adapting to digital sales and maintaining the deli's role as a political and cultural staple.

#### The Gamble Family (2024 - Present)

In January 2024, the deli was sold to restaurateur Al Gamble for an undisclosed amount. The transition emphasized continuity, with no major changes planned beyond the locks. Today,Famous 4th Street Delicatessen continues to serve as both a living museum of Jewish-American cuisine and a gathering place for storytelling, civic life, and cultural nostalgia. It remains one of the few surviving “old-school” Jewish delis in the city.

## National Recognition and Modern Era (1990s–Present)

By the late 1990s and early 2000s, Famous 4th Street Delicatessen had attracted attention well beyond Philadelphia, becoming a frequent stop for political figures and national media. During the 2000 presidential campaign, Al Gore visited the deli with Pennsylvania Governor Ed Rendell, generating national press coverage. President Barack Obama made a stop in 2010, and in 2024, Vice President Kamala Harris visited ahead of a town hall meeting. These visits contributed to the deli's longstanding reputation as a political gathering place in the city.

On Election Days, the deli often operates at full capacity from late morning until early afternoon, serving reporters, poll workers, and local officials. During this period, the deli also became associated with civic participation through the involvement of Emily Cowan, who worked at the establishment and later served on the Philadelphia Zoning Board of Adjustment, chairing the board from 2003.

Famous 4th Street Delicatessen has continued to receive recognition from national and regional media. In 2025, it was awarded a Michelin Bib Gourmand designation, which recognizes restaurants offering high-quality food at a good value.

In addition to formal awards, Famous 4th Street Delicatessen has been featured in national food and travel publications discussing notable Jewish delicatessens in the United States. Food and dining outlets such as Mashed and Cheapism have included the deli in editorial lists highlighting prominent American delicatessens. Travel publication Condé Nast Traveler has described Famous 4th Street Delicatessen as a classic Jewish deli with a long-standing presence in Philadelphia's dining landscape, noting that it can trace its history back nearly a century.

## Cultural Legacy

Over its century-long history, Famous 4th Street Delicatessen has become more than a restaurant—it is a Philadelphia cultural landmark, appearing in films, political campaigns, and local folklore.

### In film and media

The deli gained national recognition when it was featured in the 1993 Academy Award–winning film Philadelphia, starring Tom Hanks and Denzel Washington. The film's opening sequence, showcasing Philadelphia's urban landmarks, included interior shots of Famous 4th Street Delicatessen, visually linking the restaurant to the city's broader story of community and resilience.

Beyond film, the deli has frequently appeared in local and national media coverage highlighting Philadelphia's Jewish culinary heritage, its oversized sandwiches, and its role as a gathering place for residents and political figures alike. It is often described as a “living time capsule” of 20th-century deli culture, retaining its original wood booths, glass dessert cases, and framed newspaper clippings chronicling its 100-year history.

## Political Significance

The deli has long been a political hub in Philadelphia, earning the nickname "the go-to place for politicos." The Election Day tradition originated in the 1970s with Democratic leaders Pete Camiel, David Glancey, Neil Oxman, and Doc Sweitzer, who gathered for lunch and a ritualistic tea toast for good luck. This evolved into a bustling event attracting Democrats like Ed Rendell, Lynne Abraham, and Johnny Dougherty, as well as Republicans, media, and even visitors like Al Gore and Barack Obama.

David Auspitz was instrumental in this role, often called the "Mayor of 4th Street" for his community influence. Beyond the deli, he chaired Philadelphia's Zoning Board of Adjustments from 2003, mediating major developments like high-rises in Old City, and led the Queen Village Neighbors Association's zoning committee for over 20 years. He briefly served on Mayor Michael Nutter's casino task force but recused himself due to conflicts. Auspitz advocated for responsible urban planning, emphasizing community benefits and the "fabric of Philadelphia."

High-profile visitors over the decades have included:

* Vice President Al Gore, who stopped at the deli in 2000 during his presidential campaign alongside Ed Rendell, drawing large crowds and national media coverage.
* President Barack Obama, who visited the deli in 2010 during his reelection campaign, meeting with local leaders and greeting guests inside the restaurant.

* Vice President Kamala Harris, who made an appearance at the deli in 2024 ahead of a town hall event, continuing the tradition of national political figures visiting the Philadelphia icon.

## Location and Cultural Impact

Situated in Queen Village at the corner of 4th and Bainbridge streets, the deli is integral to Fabric Row, Philadelphia's historic garment district stretching along South 4th Street between Bainbridge and Catharine streets. Fabric Row originated in the late 19th century as a pushcart market for fabrics and goods, peaking in the 1930s–1960s with around 30 dealers serving tailors, designers, and immigrants. Pushcarts were outlawed in the 1950s, shifting commerce indoors, but the area faced threats from urban renewal plans like the Crosstown Expressway in the 1960s.

The deli's location tied it to this ecosystem of bakeries, fish markets, and textile shops, supporting the Jewish community's daily needs. As Fabric Row evolved—declining from 30 fabric stores to about 10 by the 2010s due to online competition and generational shifts—it incorporated modern businesses like boutiques and cafes, revitalized by initiatives from the South Street Headhouse District. The deli remains a cultural anchor in Philadelphia, symbolizing the city's Jewish heritage and resilience amid urban change.

### Symbolism and legacy

The deli's enduring appeal lies in its ability to represent the changing face of Philadelphia while remaining rooted in tradition. Its well-worn booths and towering sandwiches have witnessed generations of patrons—from working-class families and garment workers to presidents and celebrities.
