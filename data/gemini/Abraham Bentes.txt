**Abraham Ramiro Bentes** (1906–1990) was a Brazilian military officer, community leader, and historian. Rising to the rank of general in the Brazilian Army (*Exército Brasileiro*), he was one of the highest-ranking Jewish military officers in South American history. A veteran of the Brazilian Expeditionary Force (FEB) during World War II, Bentes later served as the president of the Confederation of Israelites of Brazil (CONIB) and wrote extensively on the history of Sephardic Jewish migration to the Amazon region.

---

### Early Life and Heritage
Bentes was born in Itaituba, a municipality in the northern state of Pará, Brazil. He was a descendant of Moroccan Sephardic Jews who had immigrated to the Amazon basin during the 19th century—a movement driven by economic opportunities associated with the region's rubber boom and political push factors in North Africa. 

Growing up within the Amazonian Jewish community, Bentes developed a strong interest in both public service and the preservation of his community's oral traditions. He moved south to pursue higher military education, enrolling in the Brazilian army's officer preparation academies.

---

### Military Career

#### Early Service and World War II
Bentes was commissioned into the Brazilian Army as an officer in the late 1920s. Over the subsequent decade, he served in various tactical and administrative postings across the country.

Following Brazil's declaration of war against the Axis powers in 1942, the Brazilian government formed the Brazilian Expeditionary Force (*Força Expedicionária Brasileira*, or FEB) to join Allied operations in Europe. Bentes was incorporated into the FEB staff and deployed to the Italian front in 1944. During the Italian Campaign (1944–1945), he participated in logistics, communications, and operational planning during key Allied advances in the Northern Apennines and along the Gothic Line.

#### Post-War Operations and Generalship
After the end of World War II, Bentes returned to Brazil and continued his rise through the peacetime military structure. Over the next two decades, he held senior regional commands, served in administrative directorates, and contributed to military education.

He achieved the rank of Brigadier General (*General-de-Brigada*), becoming one of the few practicing Jews to reach general officer rank in the Brazilian military. He retired from active service in the late 1960s.

---

### Civil and Historical Work

#### Jewish Community Leadership
Following his retirement from active duty, Bentes moved to Rio de Janeiro, where he became deeply involved in institutional leadership within the Brazilian Jewish community. During the 1970s and 1980s, he served as the president of the Confederation of Israelites of Brazil (CONIB), the primary central organ representing Jewish political and social interests in the country. In this capacity, he worked to foster interfaith dialogue and advocate for civil rights.

#### Historical Research and Writing
Bentes dedicated much of his later life to documenting the history of Moroccan Sephardic Jews in the Amazon—a subculture that possessed unique linguistic traits, including the use of *Haketia* (a Judeo-Spanish dialect influenced by Moroccan Arabic). 

Fearing that the unique history of the Amazonian Jewish diaspora would be forgotten as families assimilated or migrated to larger metropolitan areas like Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo, Bentes conducted genealogical research, archival work, and interviews with elder community members.

His research culminated in several published works detailing the migration patterns, economic roles, and cultural preservation of Jewish communities along the Amazon River.

---

### Personal Life and Legacy
Bentes died in Rio de Janeiro in 1990. He is remembered in Brazil as both a distinguished military officer who represented his country in combat during World War II and a crucial chronicler of Amazonian minority history. His historical studies remain primary reference texts for academic research on South American Sephardic history and the cultural evolution of the Amazon region.
